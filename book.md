*The Present Colonizes the Past*

---

I WAS INVITED to deliver the Kapila Vatsyayan Memorial Lecture 2025, and it was suggested to me that I should speak on history; on the rather contentious relationship that has now developed between professional history writing and that which goes by the name of writing history across various media, often written, it seems, by people unfamiliar with the historical method and some even with the subject itself. Barring a few exceptions, much of what they say interprets the past as they wish, even when it lacks proven evidence.

This type of distortion of the historical past has obvious implications for the interpretation of history, but its influence is not confined to interpretation alone. There are at least three other areas in which it has a determining role. These are: the concepts we use to define our cultural heritage; the propagation of inaccurate history, which is contributing to the lowering of our educational standards; and the use of history to legitimize a particular political ideology.

I believe that we need a much fuller and sharper understanding of the more problematic impact that prejudiced versions of history, driven by polemicists, have on our society's thinking. Further, we need to ask why those in authority often discourage the thoughtful exploration of historical knowledge.

Being a historian is a discipline. I will say more later about the nature of historiography, or the methodology of reaching a sound historical understanding. For now, I want to underline that even the best of historians—

perhaps **especially** the best of historians—must always be aware of their own possible biases and influences in interpreting the past. However, when narratives of the past are driven by biases, they can become fantasy. The attitude of those who write, or claim to write, history varies. Professional and academic historians, and others who train themselves to write carefully considered history, insist on using evidence of proven reliability. The larger number of others who use the media as their platform are often least concerned with evidence and make statements without checking their veracity.

Let me, however, clarify that I am not critiquing everyone who writes on historical subjects but is not a professional historian. Some writers, despite not being professional historians, contribute valuable work in historical research. They can be identified by the evidence they use as sources and their reliance on logical and rational arguments in their analyses. They generally seek serious and

well-informed discussion of their work, often engaging with professional historians and publishing in select journals.

Rather, my criticism is of those who write on historical subjects but base their writing on a populist agenda and often project events with a populist agenda and often project events through mainstream and social media. Currently, it is their views of the past that predominate in state-sponsored educational programmes.

Professional history in India over the past half-century has undergone an immense change in the range of sources, in methods of analysing those sources, and in explanations of what happened in the past. Historical research now goes far beyond dynastic history, and incorporates aspects of social, economic, cultural and environmental history. This increase in curiosity about the past and the world we live in is commendable. History is no longer just an aspect of Indology—that is,

providing information on the past. It is now an analytical discipline explaining the complexities of the past. These concern not only the 'what' and the 'where' of past events, but also the 'how' and the 'why' of what happened. Asking new questions has led to the emergence of new methods of obtaining data and investigating it. This involves fresh interpretations that often question older knowledge. When existing interpretations become invalid, they are discarded, as, for example, some long-held colonial readings of the Indian past.

These changes in historical research are exciting intellectual developments, making historical understanding a dynamic field. Nevertheless, it still relies on proven evidence, not guesses and fantasies.

HISTORY HELPS US CLARIFY what we call our cultural identity. This is central to the perception of who we are and what we are about, which in turn is pivotal to our understanding of ourselves and our cultures—our patterns of living. Historical sources from the past provide many narratives of persons and events from early times, in a variety of of what was believed as part of an oral tradition of what was believed to have happened. Some versions were then recorded in texts such as the epics—the

*Mahabharata* and the *Ramayana*—regarded more as literary compositions and religious literature than as historical writing. Some of these were consulted by historians, hoping to find nuggets of history embedded in them.

Categories of texts in Sanskrit and Prakrit that align more closely with what we today call history began to appear from the later first millennium CE and included, for example, the charitas (biographies), such as Banabhatta's *Harshacharita*, the historical biography of the king Harshavardana of Kannauj; and the vamshaavalis (chronicles) of regions and dynasties, the best-known being Kalhana's *Rajatarangini*, the history of Kashmir. Continuing the practice in the second millennium CE were the tarikh s (chronicles), written in Persian, such as the *Tarikh-i-Firoz Shahi*, the chronicle of the reign of the Sultan Firuz Shah Tughluq. In modern times, providing the evidence for any statement based on a text has become a crucial factor in reconstructions of the past that are defined as

history. Early societies, however, paid less attention not only to providing evidence but also to proving its authenticity. Working with textual evidence demanded that the historian have a further basic qualification: knowledge of the languages in which the text was written. However, when it came to explaining the past—that is, explaining why and how an event occurred, rather than just recording what happened—the deeper imprint in modern writing came initially from the colonial reading and reconstruction of the Indian past.

These readings were obsessed with a single explanation of the establishment of colonialism. European readings of Asian history accompanied the establishment of colonialism. Colonial writers maintained that the earlier rulers of Asia were despots governing impoverished peasants through an oppressive bureaucracy. The bureaucracy collected taxes in both money and in kind. Some writers maintained that the despots also had full control over the hydraulic

system of irrigation, bringing in large agricultural yields that constituted the wealth of the despot. Since such rulers did not have to claim legitimacy from the past, recourse to history was not a feature of their societies. Arguing that there was an absence of historical writing from the pre-colonial past, colonial authorities wrote their version of the history of the colony. This was effectively in accordance with the history of the colonial times, that they invented it in line with their own preconceived views as well as the requirements of the present that they wished to legitimize by claiming they had been inherited from past times.

As a result, the writing of Indian history in modern times began with colonial theories that focused mainly on dynastic and political history, and historical research now included other aspects of social, economic and political functioning. Two substantial and extensive changes were introduced into historical research: the adoption of a historical method as a procedure for research, and, where possible, the comparative use of

interdisciplinary studies. These provided alternative explanations for what happened, and why.

LET ME EXPLAIN what is meant by the **historical method**. It is the orderly investigation, step by step, of a historical problem. The first step, after deciding the theme to be researched, is the collection of data from a range of sources, some of which may not have been consulted previously or examined from diverse perspectives. The data must come from verified evidence. It cannot be hearsay, or come from posts on social media or WhatsApp, or be the wishful thinking of the writer. Furthermore, the information from

sources—texts and artefacts—should not be merely quoted and repeated but analysed afresh from various perspectives. This is particularly important where sources are in more than one language, such as Persian, Sanskrit and the regional languages for medieval history.

This is important because, for example, the nation theory of James Mill, based on the two-nation theory of James Mill (circa 1200 to 1750 CE), was earlier called the Muslim period, based on the two-nation theory of James Mill, and much of it therefore relied largely on Persian sources. Now, however, texts in Sanskrit and the regional languages are being consulted more extensively, yielding differences in historical thinking about those times and frequently contradicting the colonial and subsequent communal interpretations.

Seeking explanations about the past requires an intensive study of the sources. Who was the author of the material? Did he have an agenda? Was he conveying a point of view projected as infallible, and or a point of view projected as infallible, and was it questioned? How was the text received? After assembling the evidence, causal and explanatory connections between people and events can be investigated. These connections must draw on logical and rational arguments, not on guesses or probabilities dictated by the writer's preferences.

The introduction of interdisciplinary research resulted in historians becoming aware of methods of analysis used in other disciplines that might also be useful to their historical research. It added more data to the historical evidence, as well as prompting fresh questions. An example of this was what became the more

extensive meaning of the word 'culture'. From referring only to the thinking, writing and activities of elite groups, it came to mean the significance of introducing the idea that a society in its entirety may host many different groups—that is, patterns of living specific to cultures—that is, complex societies incorporating diverse cultural patterns had to be studied to explain why such diversity existed, the nature of the interface among different groups, and the connecting links where they existed.

For early history, in addition to archaeology, comparative analyses from linguistics and from social and economic studies are occasionally suggestive, such as in defining cultures. Currently there is also evidence from environmental studies and from genetics. All this makes the analysis of the evidence richer and more insightful. For instance, religions are no longer viewed in isolation but are seen as closely related to the

kinds of societies in which they flourished. This helps explain the forms that some beliefs have taken and the kinds of patrons they had in their propagation.

Let me give you another example of changes from earlier research. In the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, William Jones and Max Müller studied the Vedic texts, and the main study was related to language and philology—namely, investigating the structure, development and relationships of languages. These texts and their studies became the bedrock of understanding Indian society, belief and thought. Will we still be able to nurture a society whose parameters are open to debate and discussion? A university or a research institute is the apex of the education system. How can we return to exploring history as we once did, in a thoughtful and sensitively intelligent way, understanding the past more closely and reliably, observing what links it to the present, and what we might see in it as a viable future?
